What you should be able to do after this lecture

  • Distinguish between interpretability, explainability, and transparency — and identify the right tool for the right goal.
  • Apply at least two post-hoc local explanation methods on a real dataset.
  • Generate and evaluate counterfactual explanations with awareness of actionability and immutability constraints.
  • Articulate why explainability alone is insufficient for responsible ML deployment.

Potential Questions for In-Class Quiz

Short-answer (7–10 minutes each). Justify claims.

Quiz 1 — Transparency and Trust Conceptual + Applied

A data scientist produces SHAP explanations for a credit scoring model and presents them to regulators as evidence that the model is fair and trustworthy.

Question: Identify at least two reasons why transparency does not imply trust, and explain why explainability alone is insufficient for responsible ML deployment.

  • Your answer must distinguish between explainability and interpretability.
  • Your answer must reference at least one stakeholder group whose justification needs differ from the regulator's.

Quiz 2 — Shapley Axioms Applied Reasoning

Two features, race and priors_count, are used in a recidivism model. A Shapley audit finds φrace = 0 for every defendant in the test set.

Question: Does φrace = 0 imply that the model is race-neutral? Why or why not?

  • Your answer must reference the Dummy axiom and the Efficiency axiom.
  • Your answer must explain how proxy features can produce racial disparity even when φrace = 0.

Q3 — LIME Locality Short answer

A practitioner uses LIME to explain a gradient-boosted tree trained on the COMPAS cohort and reports the feature weights as a description of global model behavior.

Question: Identify the conceptual error in this interpretation and explain what LIME actually guarantees.

  • Your answer must reference local fidelity vs. global fidelity.
  • Optional: explain how the choice of neighborhood kernel affects stability of the explanation.

Quiz 4 — Counterfactual Recourse Governance Reasoning

A DiCE counterfactual for a denied loan applicant recommends changing race from minority to majority as the minimal feature change that flips the decision from rejected to approved.

Question: Is this a valid actionable recourse recommendation? What should a responsible ML practitioner do with this result?

Choose one and justify:

  1. Report it as-is — DiCE produced a mathematically valid counterfactual.
  2. Discard it — counterfactuals involving immutable attributes are always meaningless.
  3. Flag it as a governance red flag and investigate whether race is encoded as a proxy in other features.
  4. Retrain the model with race removed from the feature set and rerun DiCE.

Quiz 5 — Wachter vs Dandl Methods Short answer

Both the Wachter et al. and Dandl et al. methods generate counterfactual explanations, but they differ in how many quality criteria they optimize.

Question: Identify one criterion that the Dandl et al. method satisfies that the Wachter et al. method does not, and explain why that criterion matters for a defendant in a criminal justice setting.

  • Your answer must reference the Rashomon effect.
  • Optional: explain the role of NSGA-II in producing a Pareto front of counterfactuals.
Tip: distinguish between what a method computes, what it guarantees, and what it implies for governance. A correct computation is not automatically a defensible decision.
