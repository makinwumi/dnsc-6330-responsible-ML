What you should be able to do after this lecture

  1. Identify and classify allocative, representational, and quality-of-service harms in deployed ML systems.
  2. Apply the burden-shifting framework to evaluate whether a model's outputs constitute unlawful disparate impact.
  3. Compute and interpret AIR, ME, and SMD against the EEOC 80% Rule.
  4. Conduct a full subgroup evaluation pipeline in Python using solas-ai-disparity.
  5. Translate bias measurement findings into a documented, governance-ready audit report.

Potential Questions for In-Class Quiz

Short-answer (7-10 minutes each). Justify claims.

Quiz 1

A hiring algorithm achieves 87% overall accuracy but produces an Adverse Impact Ratio of 0.74 for Black applicants relative to White applicants, with only 35 Black applicants in the dataset. Is this sufficient evidence of disparate impact under the EEOC 80% Rule? Explain the role of sample size in your answer.

Quiz 2

The p-value for the Marginal Effect test is 0.31, meaning the disparity is not statistically significant. Does this mean the employer is free from legal risk? What does the burden-shifting framework say about practical vs. statistical significance?

Quiz 3

As the auditing data scientist, what would you recommend the employer do next? Your answer should address whether to retrain the model, adjust the decision threshold, collect more data, or restrict the model's use — and justify your choice.

Quiz 4

Suppose the employer argues the algorithm is necessary because it improves hiring efficiency by 40%. Walk through how the burden-shifting framework would evaluate this business necessity claim, and identify what the plaintiff would need to show in Step 3 to prevail.
